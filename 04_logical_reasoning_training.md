## 1. Number Series & Coding-Decoding

**Q1. Find the next number: 1, 9, 25, 49, 81, ?**

**Solution:**
*   **Step 1:** Analyze the numbers. They are all perfect squares.
    $1 = 1^2$
    $9 = 3^2$
    $25 = 5^2$
    $49 = 7^2$
    $81 = 9^2$
*   **Step 2:** Identify the pattern. The bases are consecutive odd numbers: 1, 3, 5, 7, 9.
*   **Step 3:** Find the next base. The next odd number after 9 is 11.
*   **Step 4:** Calculate the square of the next base.
    $11^2 = 121$.
*   **Final Answer:** 121.

**Q2. Find the missing number: 2, 5, 9, 19, 37, ?**

**Solution:**
*   **Step 1:** Look at the relationship between consecutive terms. Let's try multiplication since it's growing somewhat rapidly.
    $2 \times 2 = 4$ (We need 5, so add 1) $\rightarrow 2 \times 2 + 1 = 5$
    $5 \times 2 = 10$ (We need 9, so subtract 1) $\rightarrow 5 \times 2 - 1 = 9$
    $9 \times 2 = 18$ (We need 19, so add 1) $\rightarrow 9 \times 2 + 1 = 19$
    $19 \times 2 = 38$ (We need 37, so subtract 1) $\rightarrow 19 \times 2 - 1 = 37$
*   **Step 2:** Identify the alternating pattern. It's $(\text{Previous} \times 2) + 1$, then $(\text{Previous} \times 2) - 1$.
*   **Step 3:** Apply the pattern to the last known number. The last operation was $-1$, so the next must be $+1$.
    $(37 \times 2) + 1 = 74 + 1 = 75$.
*   **Final Answer:** 75.

**Q3. If in a certain language, MADRAS is coded as NBESBT, how is BOMBAY coded in that code?**

**Solution:**
*   **Step 1:** Compare the letters of the word MADRAS with its code NBESBT one by one.
    M $\rightarrow$ N (Next letter, +1)
    A $\rightarrow$ B (Next letter, +1)
    D $\rightarrow$ E (Next letter, +1)
    R $\rightarrow$ S (Next letter, +1)
    A $\rightarrow$ B (Next letter, +1)
    S $\rightarrow$ T (Next letter, +1)
*   **Step 2:** The pattern is simply replacing each letter with the immediate next letter in the alphabet.
*   **Step 3:** Apply this pattern to BOMBAY.
    B $\rightarrow$ C
    O $\rightarrow$ P
    M $\rightarrow$ N
    B $\rightarrow$ C
    A $\rightarrow$ B
    Y $\rightarrow$ Z
*   **Final Answer:** CPNCBZ.

**Q4. If Z = 52 and ACT = 48, then BAT will be equal to:**

**Solution:**
*   **Step 1:** Decode Z = 52. In the alphabet, Z is the 26th letter.
    $26 \times 2 = 52$. So the rule is (Position $\times$ 2).
*   **Step 2:** Verify the rule with ACT.
    A = 1, C = 3, T = 20.
    Sum of positions = $1 + 3 + 20 = 24$.
    Apply rule: $24 \times 2 = 48$. The rule holds.
*   **Step 3:** Apply the rule to BAT.
    B = 2, A = 1, T = 20.
    Sum of positions = $2 + 1 + 20 = 23$.
    Apply rule: $23 \times 2 = 46$.
*   **Final Answer:** 46.

**Q5. In a certain code, '15789' is written as EGKPT and '2346' is written as ALUR. How is '23549' written in that code?**

**Solution:**
*   **Step 1:** This is a direct substitution code. Write out the mapping for the given words.
    1 = E, 5 = G, 7 = K, 8 = P, 9 = T
    2 = A, 3 = L, 4 = U, 6 = R
*   **Step 2:** Map the digits of '23549' using the above key.
    2 $\rightarrow$ A
    3 $\rightarrow$ L
    5 $\rightarrow$ G
    4 $\rightarrow$ U
    9 $\rightarrow$ T
*   **Final Answer:** ALGUT.

## 2. Blood Relations & Direction Sense

**Q1. Pointing to a photograph of a boy Suresh said, "He is the son of the only son of my mother." How is Suresh related to that boy?**

**Solution:**
*   **Step 1:** Work backward from the end of the sentence. Find who "my mother" is (Suresh's mother).
*   **Step 2:** Evaluate "the only son of my mother". Since Suresh is male, the only son of his mother is Suresh himself.
*   **Step 3:** Replace that phrase in the original sentence.
    Original: "He is the son of [the only son of my mother]."
    Replaced: "He is the son of [Suresh]."
*   **Step 4:** Deduce the relationship. The boy in the photograph is Suresh's son. Therefore, Suresh is the father.
*   **Final Answer:** Father.

**Q2. A man said to a lady, "Your mother's husband's sister is my aunt." How is the lady related to the man?**

**Solution:**
*   **Step 1:** Break down the lady's side.
    "Your mother's husband" = The lady's father.
    "Your mother's husband's sister" = The lady's father's sister = The lady's Aunt.
*   **Step 2:** Connect to the man's side.
    The statement becomes: "The lady's aunt is my aunt."
*   **Step 3:** Deduce the relationship. If they share the exact same aunt (father's sister), they must be siblings (brother and sister) or cousins. In standard IndiaBix logic unless stated otherwise, assume they are siblings.
*   **Final Answer:** Sister.

**Q3. A man walks 5 km toward south and then turns to the right. After walking 3 km he turns to the left and walks 5 km. Now in which direction is he from the starting place?**

**Solution:**
*   **Step 1:** Draw the path. Start at the origin (0,0).
*   **Step 2:** "Walks 5 km toward south". Position is 5 km South.
*   **Step 3:** "Turns to the right". Facing south, a right turn makes you face West.
*   **Step 4:** "Walks 3 km". Position is 5 km South, 3 km West.
*   **Step 5:** "Turns to the left". Facing west, a left turn makes you face South again.
*   **Step 6:** "Walks 5 km". Add 5 more km South.
*   **Step 7:** Calculate total displacement.
    Total South = $5 + 5 = 10 \text{ km}$.
    Total West = $3 \text{ km}$.
    Since he is south and west of the starting point, the direction is South-West.
*   **Final Answer:** South-West.

**Q4. Rahul put his timepiece on the table in such a way that at 6 P.M. hour hand points to North. In which direction the minute hand will point at 9.15 P.M.?**

**Solution:**
*   **Step 1:** Determine normal clock directions.
    At 6:00, the hour hand normally points to 6 (South).
*   **Step 2:** Determine the rotation of the clock.
    If the hand at 6 points North instead of South, the entire clock has been rotated by 180 degrees.
*   **Step 3:** Find the normal direction for 9:15.
    At 9:15, the minute hand points at 3. Normally, the 3 position is East.
*   **Step 4:** Apply the 180-degree rotation.
    East rotated 180 degrees becomes West.
*   **Final Answer:** West.

**Q5. P, Q, R, S, T, U, V and W are sitting round the circle and are facing the centre: P is second to the right of T who is the neighbour of R and V. S is not the neighbour of P. V is the neighbour of U. Q is not between S and W. W is not between U and S. Which two of the following are not neighbours?**

**Solution:**
*   **Step 1:** Start with the most concrete information.
    "T is the neighbour of R and V" $\rightarrow$ The block is R-T-V or V-T-R.
*   **Step 2:** "P is second to the right of T".
    Assume T faces the center. Right is counter-clockwise.
    If V-T-R, P is where R is (conflict).
    So it must be R-T-V. Then P is one space right of V.
    Current arrangement: R - T - V - (empty) - P.
*   **Step 3:** "V is the neighbour of U".
    V's neighbors are T and the empty space. So the empty space is U.
    Current arrangement: R - T - V - U - P.
*   **Step 4:** "S is not the neighbour of P".
    We have 3 spots left. S cannot be next to P.
*   **Step 5:** "W is not between U and S".
    U is next to P. W must be carefully placed.
*   **Step 6:** Following all these constraints, the only valid circle reading clockwise is:
    R, T, V, U, W, S, Q, P.

## 3. Syllogisms

**Q1. Statements: All dogs are cats. Some cats are rats.**
**Conclusions: I. Some dogs are rats. II. No dog is rat.**

**Solution:**
*   **Step 1:** Draw the Venn diagram.
    Draw a circle for "Dogs" completely inside a circle for "Cats".
*   **Step 2:** Draw "Some cats are rats".
    Overlap a circle for "Rats" with the "Cats" circle.
*   **Step 3:** Analyze the overlap possibilities.
    The "Rats" circle MUST overlap with "Cats". But does it overlap with "Dogs"? It *might*, but it doesn't *have* to.
*   **Step 4:** Evaluate conclusions.
    Conclusion I ("Some dogs are rats") is not definitely true.
    Conclusion II ("No dog is rat") is not definitely true either.
*   **Step 5:** Check for complementary pairs. "Some" and "No" with the same subjects form a complementary pair. Since we don't know if they overlap or not, it must be exactly one or the other.
*   **Final Answer:** Either I or II follows.

**Q2. Statements: Some buildings are chalks. No chalk is toffee.**
**Conclusions: I. No building is toffee. II. All chalks are buildings.**

**Solution:**
*   **Step 1:** Draw Venn Diagram.
    Overlap "Buildings" and "Chalks".
    Draw "Toffee" completely separate from "Chalks".
*   **Step 2:** Evaluate Conclusion I ("No building is toffee").
    While Toffee cannot touch Chalk, it *could* touch the part of the Building circle that is outside the Chalk circle. Thus, it's not definitely true that NO building is a toffee. Conclusion I is false.
*   **Step 3:** Evaluate Conclusion II ("All chalks are buildings").
    The statement says "Some buildings are chalks". This does not mean ALL chalks are buildings. Conclusion II is false.
*   **Final Answer:** Neither I nor II follows.

**Q3. Statements: All windows are doors. No door is a wall.**
**Conclusions: I. Some windows are walls. II. No wall is a door.**

**Solution:**
*   **Step 1:** Draw Venn Diagram.
    Draw "Windows" completely inside "Doors".
    Draw "Walls" completely separate from "Doors".
*   **Step 2:** Evaluate Conclusion I ("Some windows are walls").
    Since Walls cannot touch Doors, and Windows are trapped inside Doors, Walls can NEVER touch Windows. Conclusion I is definitely false.
*   **Step 3:** Evaluate Conclusion II ("No wall is a door").
    The statement says "No door is a wall". This is perfectly reversible. If A is not B, then B is not A. Conclusion II is definitely true.
*   **Final Answer:** Only Conclusion II follows.

**Q4. Statements: Some papers are pens. All the pencils are pens.**
**Conclusions: I. Some pens are pencils. II. Some pens are papers.**

**Solution:**
*   **Step 1:** Analyze the statements logically (without drawing if possible).
*   **Step 2:** "All the pencils are pens".
    If every pencil is a pen, then naturally, some of the pens in existence are those pencils. Conclusion I ("Some pens are pencils") is a direct derivation and is true.
*   **Step 3:** "Some papers are pens".
    Overlap statements are reversible. If A overlaps B, then B overlaps A. Conclusion II ("Some pens are papers") is directly true.
*   **Final Answer:** Both I and II follow.

**Q5. Statements: All buses are cars. All ships are cars.**
**Conclusions: I. All cars are ships. II. All buses are ships.**

**Solution:**
*   **Step 1:** Draw Venn Diagram.
    Draw a large circle for "Cars".
    Draw "Buses" completely inside "Cars".
    Draw "Ships" completely inside "Cars".
*   **Step 2:** Evaluate Conclusion I ("All cars are ships").
    Cars is the larger category. It contains buses too. So all cars are not ships. Conclusion I is false.
*   **Step 3:** Evaluate Conclusion II ("All buses are ships").
    We know both are inside Cars, but we don't know if the "Buses" circle and "Ships" circle touch each other at all. Conclusion II is not definitely true.
*   **Final Answer:** Neither I nor II follows.
